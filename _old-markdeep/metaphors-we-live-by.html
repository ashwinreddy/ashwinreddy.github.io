**_Metaphors We Live By_** In this lucid little book, George Lakoff and Mark Johnson are concerned to show, with plenty of examples, how crucial metaphor is to language, thought, action, and even truth itself. Consider the metaphor Argument is War: > Your claims are _indefensible_.
> He _attacked every weak point_ in my argument.
> His criticisms were _right on target_.
> I _demolished_ his argument.
> I've never _won_ an argument with him.
> If you use that _strategy_, he'll _wipe you out_.
> He _shot down_ all of my arguments. Or Time is Money: > How do you _spend_ your time these days?
> That flat tire _cost_ me an hour.
> I've _invested_ a lot of time in her.
> You need to _budget_ your time.
> Is that _worth your while_?
> He's living on _borrowed_ time.
> You don't _use_ your time _profitably_.
Most of us speak like this without a second thought, but you will agree that Argument is War and Time is Money are not observable facts about the world. They are but partial structures for foggy concepts. (Under metaphor, Lakoff and Johnson subsume personification and metonymy.) Another kind of metaphor, weaker than structural, orients a concept by placing it along some axis. Having Control or Force is Up; Being Subject to Control or Force is Down > He's in a _superior_ position. He's at the _height_ of his power. He ranks _above_ me in strength. He _fell_ from power. He is _low man_ on the totem pole. > Lakoff and Johnson explain that this metaphor is based on physical experience. The victor in a fight is typically bigger and on top. Social status or power is then also up by correlation with physical power. And more broadly good is up because control and power are up, along with happiness, health, and life. In this way, metaphors within a culture tend to cohere and build upon each other. Change the culture, and the metaphors change too. YouTube and Netflix accustom us to seeing time move left to right. The ancient Romans, by way of contrast, had time move above to below.
