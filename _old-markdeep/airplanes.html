**On Airplanes** It is curious that a poet, a priest, and a philosopher -- as if this were the start of a bad joke -- all picked the same thing when reflecting on the times: the airplane. _The great tragedy of modern art_, according to writer Vladimir Nabokov, _is that_ "an artist cannot look at an airplane and see anything but a plane."[^nabokov] The Zen monk Shunryu Suzuki said > For instance, we create airplanes and highways. And when we repeat "I create, I create, I create," soon we forget who is actually the "I" which creates the various things; we soon forget about God. This is the danger of human culture.[^zen] > The tragedy that we cannot see planes as anything more than planes and the danger of forgetting who (or what) creates them are not unrelated. The philosopher Martin Heidegger was deeply sensitive to both the artistic tragedy and the spiritual danger when he composed these sentences: > [A]n airliner that stands on the runway is surely an object. Certainly. We can represent the machine so. But then it conceals itself as to what and how it is. Revealed, it stands on the taxi strip only as standing-reserve, inasmuch as it is ordered to insure the possibility of transportation. For this it must be in its whole structure and in every one of its constituent parts itself on call for duty, i.e., ready for takeoff.[^heidegger] > Heidegger says the essence of technology lies in enframing. The problem, Heidegger qualifies, is not that the plane is treated as just another object. On some level, it is. But this subject-object type analysis comes in too late; it totally passes over the way in which the plane reveals itself to us. Nor is the concern here about planes as instruments, mere means to an end, which could be said of any technology. No, what distinguishes modern technology is immediate availability or, in Heidegger's jargon, standing-reserve. Consider, for example, the frustration inherent when some maintenance issue delays a flight. Modern technology thus challenges us to order more and more of the world into standing-reserve. This Heidegger calls enframing (_Gestell_). In it lies the danger, for we may forget there are other ways of revealing. Heidegger's solution is to look to art. Modern technology in Heidegger becomes something ominous, looming over humans and commanding them. This is because Heidegger never actually explains where technology comes from, only how it reveals itself to humans. There is something missing in his account. Félix Guattari comments that Heidegger's enframing > is essentially operated by man and understood in terms of a universal operation, travelling, flying...But does this "standing-reserve" of the machine really reside in an already-there, in terms of eternal truths, revealed to the being of man? In fact the machine speaks to the machine before speaking to man [^enframing] > He illustrates with the Concorde. > -- a diagrammatic Universe with plans of theoretical "feasability;"
