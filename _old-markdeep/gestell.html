**Footnote on _Gestell_** In "The Question Concerning Technology," philosopher Martin Heidegger names the essence of modern technology, along with its danger, _Ge-stell_. He notes the strangeness of the word: it ordinarily means a frame, as in a bookshelf or a skeleton. Yet he continues to use it, signaling deliberate wordplay. Down the rabbit hole we go, then. First note that Heidegger hyphenates the word, splitting the noun into its prefix _Ge-_ and root verb _stellen_, which means to place, put, or set up. From it we get _herstellen_ and _darstellen_, to produce and to present. But Heidegger also wants _stellen_ here to abut _herausfordern_, that is, to challenge or provoke. As for the prefix, _Ge-_ indicates collection or gathering, as in _Gebirg_ (mountain range) from _Berg_ (mountain). Thus Heidegger is being quite literal when he says _Ge-stell_ > means the gathering together of the setting-upon (_Stellens_) that sets upon (_stellt_) man, i.e., challenges (_herausfordern_) him forth, to reveal the actual, in the mode of ordering (_Bestellens_), as standing-reserve (_Bestand_). > _Ge-stell_ is a way of revealing. William Lovitt translates _Ge-stell_ as "enframing" while Andrew Mitchell takes it as "positionality." Jacques Derrida's proposal is _correction_, an elision of "co-erecting."[^derrida] [^derrida]: _Theory and Practice_, p. 124
